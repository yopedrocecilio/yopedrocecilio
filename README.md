### `> whoami`

i'm pedro. brazilian. 31. moved to san francisco to build things for builders.

i spend most of my time thinking about why the world's most talented people are still invisible — and then building the infrastructure to fix that. on the way, i accidentally became obsessed with community, AI, vibe coding, and the question of what "work" even means when machines can do most of it.

i don't have all the answers. but i have 3,000+ builders who trust me, strong opinions held loosely, and an unreasonable amount of conviction that this matters.

if you're reading this you're probably a builder, a founder, or a recruiter. either way — welcome, grab a seat.

---

### what i'm building

🌎 **[BeGlobal](https://beglobal.work)** — a community of the world's best builders.

3,000+ startup-proven engineers, mostly from Latin America. we place elite talent into early-stage teams in 48h — bootstrapped, profitable, growing. but the long game was always the community itself. a living network of people who share the same values: craft, speed, ownership, taste. placement is how we sustain it. **the community is why it exists.**

⚡ **[UNDV](https://undv.com)** — where AI builders become visible. `pre-launch`

identity, discovery, connection, monetization — for the new class shipping with lovable, replit, v0, claude, cursor. your work becomes your reputation. creation becomes currency. not a marketplace. not a job board. something new.

---

### things i believe

> talent is everywhere. access is not. that's a design problem, not a law of nature.

- in a post-AGI world, the scarce thing isn't skill — it's taste, judgment, and knowing *what* to build
- community compounds in ways software alone never will
- the best people i've met were in places the market wasn't looking
- proof of work > proof of credentials. every single time
- builders are becoming a social class. the infrastructure hasn't caught up yet
- the question isn't "will AI replace us?" — it's "what kind of person becomes *more* powerful when AI does everything else?"

---

### on builders

i've spent years talking to thousands of engineers across latin america. the pattern is clear: the best ones don't have the best resumes. they have the best side projects, the sharpest opinions on architecture, and a kind of hunger you can't fake.

the world is sleeping on these people. not for long.

### thoughts i keep coming back to

<details>
<summary><i>"the meaning of life is just to be alive. it is so plain and so obvious and so simple. and yet, everybody rushes around in a great panic as if it were necessary to achieve something beyond themselves."</i> — alan watts</summary>
<br>
i think about this a lot. especially as a founder. it's easy to let ambition eat your life. the best work i've done came from seasons where i was fully present — not optimizing, not performing, just building because the work felt alive. the goal isn't to be impressive. the goal is to feel fully in it.
</details>

<details>
<summary><i>"the people who succeed are irrationally passionate about something."</i> — naval</summary>
<br>
naval got a lot of things right, but this is the one that shaped me most. rationality is overrated as a startup virtue. the founders and builders i admire most aren't calculated — they're obsessed. they picked something that bothered them enough to spend years on it. for me that's global talent and access. it's not rational. it's personal.
</details>

<details>
<summary><i>"a great company is a conspiracy to change the world."</i> — peter thiel</summary>
<br>
i don't agree with thiel on everything but this line is burned into my brain. the best companies don't feel like businesses. they feel like movements with a point of view. beglobal started as a recruiting company but became a community because the people inside it cared about something beyond the transaction. that's the conspiracy. people who choose the same mission independently.
</details>

<details>
<summary>on life in san francisco</summary>
<br>
sf is the only city i've been where you can sit next to a stranger at a coffee shop and within 10 minutes you're both drawing architecture diagrams on a napkin. it's intense, sometimes exhausting, and probably the most expensive place to discover that the meaning of life might actually be a long walk with no AirPods. i love it here. and i miss São Paulo every day. you can hold both.
</details>

<details>
<summary>on building from brazil</summary>
<br>
in brazil you learn resourcefulness. how to build with constraints. how bureaucracy kills momentum and that most problems are distribution problems, not invention problems. in SF you learn conviction. how the right idea with the right energy attracts everything it needs. the combination is lethal — the hunger of someone from a place where nothing was guaranteed, with the ambition of a city where people are genuinely trying to change the world. i want that for every builder in our community. not escape. expansion.
</details>

<details>
<summary>on freedom, work, and the point of it all</summary>
<br>
naval says "retirement is when you stop sacrificing today for an imaginary tomorrow." i think about that constantly. i don't want to build a company just to exit. i want to build something i'd still choose on a random tuesday. something that makes my life bigger, not smaller. freedom isn't the absence of work. it's doing work that you'd do even if you didn't have to. that's what i'm optimizing for.
</details>
